Noh Theatre

Origins

Introduced in 9th century from China

Evolved from 'sangaku' (acrobats) and 'dengaku' (celebration)

Original performers were generational and highly respected

Brought to present-day form by actors Kan'ami Kiyotsugu and Zeami Motokiyo

Performances are dictated by 'Jo-Ha-Kyu', which defines Noh practice from plays to music incorporated

Performers

Training begins as early as childhood

Actors play as either 'Shite' (protagonist), 'Waki' (companion), 'Kyogen' (interlude), or 'Hayashi' (instrumentalists)

Performance

Distinctive masks of cypress used by actors to deliberately portray emotions

Open stage with no obstructions allow for complete audience engagement

'Shozoku' (silk robes) and wigs/hats used to portray characters

A 'hayashi' ensemble and chorus are consistantly playing for musical accompaniment

Subjects

'Genzai': linear timeline of human events

'Mugen': non-linear timeline, with flashbacks, tales of supernatural beings

'Ryokake': hybrid with first act Genzai and second Mugen

Famous Works

Aoi nu Ue (Lady Aoi)

Aya no Tsuzumi (The Damask Drum)

Hagoromo (The Feather Mantle)
